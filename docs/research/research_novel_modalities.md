## 1. Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs)

### Background

ADCs are engineered therapeutics composed of three components: (1) a tumor-antigen-targeting monoclonal antibody, (2) a cytotoxic payload, and (3) a chemical linker connecting the two. The concept — "magic bullet" targeted delivery — traces to Paul Ehrlich's early 20th-century hypothesis. Commercial ADC development started in earnest in the 1990s, with the first FDA approval (Mylotarg) in 2000.

### 1.2 ADC Technology Generations

| Generation | Time Period | Characteristics | Linker | Key Examples | Limitations |
|------------|------------|-----------------|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **1st Gen** | ~1990s–2010 | Non-optimized random conjugation; heterogeneous DAR; labile linkers; poor efficacy/safety | Acid-cleavable hydrazone (unstable in circulation) | Mylotarg (2000); BR96-Dox | Premature payload release; systemic toxicity; poor TI; most candidates failed |
| **2nd Gen** | ~2010–2017 | Improved cleavable linkers (cathepsin-B); cysteine-based conjugation; better DAR control (4–4); validated payloads (MMAE, DM1) | Enzyme-cleavable (Val-Cit); non-cleavable (SMCC) | Adcetris (2011); Kadcyla (2013); Besponsa (2017) | Heterogeneous conjugation; still limited payload potency range; high toxicity profiles |
| **3rd Gen** | ~2017–2022 | Site-specific conjugation; controlled DAR; diverse payloads; improved TI | Enzyme-cleavable; next-gen cleavable (tetrapeptide, glucuronide) | Enhertu (2019, DAR 8); Trodelvy (2020); Padcev (2019) | "Bystander effect" exploited intentionally (T-DXd); complexity of site-specific chemistry |
| **4th Gen / Next-Gen** | ~2022–present | Bispecific ADCs; biparatopic targeting; dual-payload ADCs; non-oncology ADCs; immunostimulatory ADCs (ISACs); novel warheads (STING agonists, TLR agonists) | Highly stable cleavable; bioorthogonal; enzyme-triggered | SKB264, RC88 (bispecific); ISAC platforms | Regulatory novelty; complex manufacturing; higher COGS |

**Key Linker/Payload Innovations:**
- **Val-Cit (Valine-Citrulline) linker:** Dominant enzyme-cleavable linker since 2nd gen; cleaved by lysosomal cathepsin B intracellularly
- **Tetrapeptide linker:** Used in Enhertu/Dato-DXd; faster enzymatic cleavage, optimized for high DAR
- **GalNAc-cleavable linkers:** Emerging for liver-targeted ADCs
- **DXd payload:** Exatecan derivative (TOP1 inhibitor); enables high DAR (8) with good TI; bystander killing effect — paradigm-shifting for HER2-low indications
- **Microtubule inhibitors:** MMAE, MMAF, DM1, DM4 — dominant class in approvals; inhibit tubulin polymerization
- **DNA-damaging agents:** Calicheamicin (DNA strand break), PBD dimers (DNA crosslink), duocarmycin (DNA alkylation)
- **Topoisomerase I inhibitors:** SN-38 (Trodelvy), DXd (Enhertu/Dato-DXd) — increasingly dominant in solid tumors

**DAR Evolution:**
- 1st gen: DAR 2–3 (heterogeneous)
- 2nd gen: DAR 3.5–4 (more controlled)
- 3rd gen: DAR 7–8 (high-DAR enabled by stable linker, e.g., T-DXd DAR 8)

## 2. Gene Therapies

### Background

Gene therapy involves delivery of genetic material into cells to treat or prevent disease. Vectors include viral (AAV, lentiviral, retroviral, adenoviral, HSV) and non-viral systems. Applications span: gene replacement (add functional copy of a defective gene), gene silencing, gene editing (CRISPR/Cas9, base editing), and ex vivo genetic modification of stem or immune cells.

The field experienced two major "waves": an early wave (1990s–2002) with enthusiasm followed by the Jesse Gelsinger death (1999) and leukemia cases in ADA-SCID trials (2002–2003) setback; and a second wave (2012–present) driven by improved vector design, better understanding of immunogenicity, and first regulatory approvals.

### 2.1 Key Historical Milestones

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1972 | Friedmann & Roblin theoretical paper | First formal proposal for gene therapy as therapeutic approach |
| 1990 | First human gene therapy trial (ADA-SCID, W. French Anderson, NIH) | Used γ-retroviral vector; modest clinical success; milestone of the field |
| 1993 | CFTR gene therapy trials begin | Early attempt at lung gene therapy; limited success due to immunity |
| 1999 | Jesse Gelsinger death (adenoviral vector, UPenn) | Major setback; first death in a gene therapy trial; reshaped regulatory oversight |
| 2002–2003 | Leukemia cases in X-SCID retroviral trial (Fischer, Paris) | Insertional mutagenesis from γ-retrovirus; drove shift to self-inactivating lentiviral vectors |
| 2003 | Gendicine approved in China | First commercial gene therapy (adenoviral p53 for head/neck cancer); not accepted by EMA/FDA |
| 2007 | SIN lentiviral vector replaces γ-retroviral for most ex vivo applications | Safety improvement for hematopoietic gene therapy |
| 2009 | ALD lentiviral gene therapy proof-of-concept (Bluebird Bio/Paris) | First successful lentiviral gene therapy for brain disease |
| 2012 | **Glybera (alipogene tiparvovec) — EMA approval** | First gene therapy approved in Western world; AAV1-LPL for LPLD; withdrawn 2017 (commercial reasons) |
| 2014 | Choroideremia AAV trial (MacLaren, Oxford) | Early success for ocular AAV gene therapy |
| 2015 | **Imlygic (T-VEC) — FDA approval** | First oncolytic viral therapy; HSV-1 expressing GM-CSF; melanoma |
| 2016 | Strimvelis — EMA approval (GSK/Orchard) | Ex vivo γ-retroviral ADA-SCID therapy; first approved for a primary immunodeficiency |

| 2019 | **Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec) — FDA approval** | AAV9-SMN1; SMA type 1; most expensive drug ever at launch ($2.1M) |
| 2020 | CRISPR/Cas9 used in first in vivo base-editing human trial | Carbapenem-resistant bacterial infection; later: LNP-delivered CRISPR for hATTR |
| 2022 | Multiple simultaneous approvals (see table below) | Record year: Zynteglo, Skysona, Hemgenix, Roctavian, Adstiladrin, Lenmeldy |

| Vector Type | Key Properties | Tropism/Organ Targets | Examples |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------|----------|
| **AAV (Adeno-Associated Virus)** | Non-integrating; episomal; low immunogenicity; serotype determines tropism | AAV9: CNS, muscle; AAV2: retina; AAV5: liver; AAVrh74: muscle; AAVrh10: brain | Zolgensma (AAV9), Luxturna (AAV2), Hemgenix (AAV5), Elevidys (AAVrh74), Beqvez (AAVrh74var) |

| **HSV-1 (Herpes Simplex)** | Oncolytic strains; large cargo; neurotropic | Skin (topical), tumor (intratumoral), neuron | Imlygic (melanoma), Vyjuvek (skin), DB-OTO (hearing) |
| **CRISPR/Cas9 (ex vivo)** | Genome editing; precise modification; ex vivo HSC | Hematopoietic stem cells | Casgevy (Vertex/CRISPR Therapeutics) |
| **Base Editing** | Precise single nucleotide change; no DSB | CNS (LNP or AAV-delivered), liver, eye | Beam Therapeutics, Prime Medicine (preclinical/early clinical) |

**Organ Targeting Summary:**
- **Liver:** Largest target (metabolic diseases, hemophilia); AAV5/AAV8/LNP-preferred
- **Eye:** Well-established AAV2 efficacy; retinal disorders (RPE65, RPGR, CEP290)
- **CNS/Brain:** AAV9/AAVrh10 intrathecal/IV or direct stereotactic injection
- **Muscle:** AAV8/AAVrh74 for DMD, SMA; IV delivery
- **HSC (ex vivo):** Lentiviral; hemoglobinopathies, lysosomal storage disorders, immune deficiencies
- **Skin:** HSV-1 topical (Vyjuvek); dermatological genetic conditions
- **Tumor (oncolytic):** IT or IV; armed oncolytic viruses (HSV, vaccinia, adeno)

## 3. Cell Therapies (CAR-T and Beyond)

### Background

Cell therapies involve administering living cells into a patient to treat disease. The field spans autologous (patient's own cells) and allogeneic (donor "off-the-shelf") approaches. CAR-T (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell) therapy emerged as the dominant modality after 2017. Beyond CAR-T, the space includes TIL (tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes), TCR-T (TCR-engineered T cells), NK (Natural Killer) cell therapies, and stem/progenitor cell therapies.

## 4. RNA Therapeutics

### Background

RNA therapeutics encompass several distinct drug classes that act at the RNA level rather than the protein level, enabling precision targeting of genes previously considered "undruggable." The field began with antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) in the 1970s–1980s theoretically, and the first clinical approval came in 1998. siRNA and mRNA-based therapies emerged from 1998's Fire & Mello RNAi discovery (Nobel 2006). mRNA therapeutics had a transformative breakthrough with COVID-19 vaccines in 2021.

### 4.1 Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASOs)

ASOs are short single-stranded synthetic DNA/RNA oligonucleotides (15–25 nt) that bind complementary mRNA sequences via Watson-Crick base pairing, triggering RNase H-mediated mRNA degradation (phosphorothioate/gapmer design), steric block of translation, or splice modulation.

**Backbone Chemistry Generations:**
- **1st gen:** Phosphorothioate (PS) backbone — improved stability, RNase H competent; high protein binding/toxicity
- **2nd gen:** Mixed backbone "gapmers" with 2'-MOE or 2'-O-Me flanks + PS core; better safety/potency
- **3rd gen:** LNA (locked nucleic acid), cET (constrained ethyl), BNA; much higher affinity; subcutaneous delivery possible; GalNAc conjugation for liver targeting

### 4.2 siRNA Therapeutics

siRNA (small interfering RNA) are double-stranded RNA molecules (21–23 nt) that enter the RISC (RNA-induced silencing complex) machinery to guide sequence-specific mRNA degradation. Mechanism: post-transcriptional gene silencing via Argonaute 2 (Ago2) cleavage of target mRNA.

**Key innovation — GalNAc conjugation:** GalNAc (N-acetylgalactosamine) triantennary ligand targets ASGPR (asialoglycoprotein receptor) on hepatocytes; enables subcutaneous delivery with 90%+ liver selectivity and very long duration of action (1–3 doses per year); pioneered by Alnylam.

### 4.3 mRNA Therapeutics and Vaccines

mRNA therapeutics use synthetic messenger RNA that is translated into therapeutic proteins inside cells. Key advantages: rapid design (no viral vectors), transient expression (safety), scalable manufacturing, protein targets otherwise inaccessible. The LNP delivery platform is critical: lipid nanoparticles protect mRNA from degradation and facilitate cellular uptake.

### 4.4 RNA Therapeutics Delivery Systems

| System | Mechanism | Key Applications | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|------------|-------------|
| **LNP (Lipid Nanoparticles)** | Ionizable lipid + DSPC + cholesterol + PEG-lipid; pH-responsive endosomal escape | siRNA (Patisiran), mRNA vaccines, mRNA therapeutics, in vivo gene editing | Scalable; validated; versatile payload (siRNA, mRNA, CRISPR RNA); IV or IM delivery | Liver tropism dominant; cold chain; IV delivery for liver; IM for vaccines |
| **GalNAc Conjugation** | Triantennary N-acetylgalactosamine targets ASGPR on hepatocytes | siRNA (Givosiran, Lumasiran, Inclisiran, Vutrisiran); ASO (Eplontersen, Olezarsen) | Hepatocyte-specific; SC injection; long half-life (1–3 doses/year); no cold chain | Liver-restricted; ASGPR saturation at high doses |
| **Naked/Unformulated** | ASOs with PS backbone self-deliver to kidneys, CNS via intrathecal | ASO (Nusinersen [IT], many naked SC ASOs) | Simple; no carrier needed for some tissues | Low systemic bioavailability; tissue-restricted |
| **Lipoplexes / Polymeric NPs** | Cationic lipid or polymer complexation | Investigational; pulmonary delivery | Pulmonary targeting potential | Toxicity concerns; lower efficacy vs. LNP |
| **Exosomes / Extracellular Vesicles** | Natural vesicles for RNA cargo | Preclinical/early clinical | Natural targeting; low immunogenicity | Scalability; standardization challenges |
| **GalNAc-LNP** | GalNAc-lipid conjugated LNP | Next-gen; hepatocyte targeting with LNP payload capacity | Combine liver specificity with mRNA capacity | In IND-enabling studies (VERVE-102) |
| **Antibody-conjugated LNP** | Antibody-targeted LNP for T-cell or tumor delivery | In vivo CAR-T generation (preclinical); tumor mRNA delivery | Extrahepatic targeting | Early stage; manufacturing complexity |
| **Morpholino Antisense (PMO)** | Neutral backbone PMO; steric block | DMD exon skipping (Exondys, Vyondys, Amondys, Viltepso) | No RNase H; safe; splice modulation | IV delivery; limited tissue penetration |

### 6.3 Convergence Themes

1. **Modality convergence:** ADCs now use PROTAC-warheads; siRNA loaded into LNPs targeting tumors; CAR-T cells delivering gene-silencing payloads. Boundaries between modalities blurring.

2. **Off-the-shelf imperative:** Autologous CAR-T and TIL face manufacturing bottlenecks (weeks-long production, high COGS, vein-to-vein time). Allogeneic CAR-T (iPSC-derived), CAR-NK, and fast-manufactured CAR-T (Point-of-care, 24-48h) are the next major transition.

3. **Autoimmune frontier:** Cell therapies (CAR-T) and RNA therapeutics (siRNA) are rapidly expanding into autoimmune disease — a patient population far larger than oncology, potentially a bigger commercial opportunity than cancer.

4. **Target richness:** 670 targets in active drug development as of 2024 (up from 375 in 2021). Novel targets like CLDN18.2, CD7, B7-H3, and GPRC5D now appear across multiple modalities simultaneously (ADC + CAR-T + bispecific).

5. **China's rising influence:** China now leads gene therapy trial initiations (since 2016) and ADC development (812 active trials vs. 524 US). Chinese biotechs drive CLDN18.2, ROR1, TROP-2, and novel ADC targets.

6. **Regulatory acceleration:** FDA Accelerated Approval, Breakthrough Therapy Designation, RMAT (Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy) designation, and rare pediatric disease vouchers are systematically shortening development timelines for novel modalities.

7. **Price and access:** These modalities command unprecedented prices — Zolgensma ($2.1M; now exceeded by Lenmeldy at $4.25M), ADC courses at $150–200K/year, CAR-T at $400–500K/course — creating payer and health system access challenges.
